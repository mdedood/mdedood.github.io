1. Introduction - Ethics in Science


A great inspiration for this module part 1A is an online course on scientific ethics, by Anthony Carpi and Anne E. Egger. I have included shortened versions of their text and have given a few examples that are particularly relevant for the physical sciences.


Ethics is a set of moral obligations that define right and wrong in our practices and decisions. Scientific ethics calls for honesty and integrity in all stages of scientific practice, from data collection to publication and beyond. Many of the ethical principles in science relate to the production of unbiased scientific knowledge, which is critical when others try to build upon or extend research findings. The open publication of data, peer review, replication, and collaboration required by the scientific ethic all help to keep science moving forward by validating research findings and confirming or raising questions about results.


Some breaches of the ethical standards, such as fabrication of data, are dealt with by the scientific community through means similar to ethical breaches in other disciplines – removal from a job, for example. But less obvious challenges to the ethical standard occur more frequently. Sometimes scientists simply make mistakes that may appear to be ethical breaches. In this course we focus on good use of data analysis methods to avoid erronous interpretation of scientific data.


Scientists have long maintained an informal system of ethics and guidelines for conducting research. Documented ethical guidelines did not develop until the mid-twentieth century. This started after a series of well-publicized ethical breaches and war crimes during the second world war. Ethical standards that help guide scientific research and ensure that research efforts (and researchers) abide by several core principles.


Core principles of an ethical standard for scientific research


1. Honesty in reporting of scientific data;
2. Careful transcription and analysis of scientific results to avoid error;
3. Independent analysis and interpretation of results that is based on data and not on the influence of external sources;
4. Open sharing of methods, data, and interpretations through publication and presentation;
5. Sufficient validation of results through replication and collaboration with peers;
6. Proper crediting of sources of information, data, and ideas;
7. Moral obligations to society in general, and, in some disciplines, responsibility in weighing the rights of human and animal subjects.

Note: This list is likely incomplete. These principles should be discussed with peers, colleagues, supervisors, independent experts, etc.

4. Case study: Cold Fusion

Nuclear fusion creates four times more energy than nuclear fission, and four million times more energy than a chemical reaction, such as the burning of gas, oil or coal. A single glass of sea water could produce the same amount of energy as burning a barrel of oil, and it would release no waste. Fusion could supply all of mankind's energy needs without contributing to global warming.


In 1989 Pons and Fleischman, then at the university of Utah, announced room-temperature nuclear fusion in a jar filled with heavy water (water comprised of deuterium and oxygen atoms). They then passed an electric current from a palladium cathode through the heavy water, and the palladium absorbed deuterium atoms, which according to Pons and Fleischmann, forced them to fuse, and generated heat and neutrons.


Scientists around the world tried to replicate the experiment. A team led by Dr. Nathan Lewis at the California Institute of Technology used more sensitive equipment than the Utah team had used, and they found no fusion had taken place. They also found that no neutrons, gamma rays, tritium or helium were emitted, as the Utah team had claimed. The interpretation of the experiments and the claims were severely criticized at the APS meeting in Baltimore (see the N.Y. Times article: Physicists Debunk Claim Of a New Kind of Fusion, May 3, 1989 on the topic. They also made it to the cover of Time magazine, something that only very few scientists have achieved.

For an in depth study see the Cold fusion: A case study for scientific behavior website by the understanding science team at the University of Berkely
